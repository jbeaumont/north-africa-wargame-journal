# Journal Entry — Turn 1, Week of 9 September 1940

---

The war in North Africa begins not with a thunderclap but with a logistics spreadsheet.

I have spent the better part of three hours just *deploying* units and tracing supply lines, and already the situation is, to put it charitably, a catastrophe — primarily for the Italians, whom I am nominally trying to lead to glory. Marshal Graziani's 10th Army has arrived on the map at hex 1405, and I have never seen a headquarters unit look so lonely. The Cirene and Marmarica divisions are filing in behind him along the Libyan coastal road, fresh reinforcements stepping off the trucks and immediately discovering that nobody thought to bring enough water.

Six Italian infantry regiments are critically short of water on *Turn 1*. The 125th and 126th Cirene, the 115th and 116th Marmarica, and — God help them — the 141st and 142nd Catanzaro, who were already here and apparently drinking sand. The Catanzaro's 141st is fully out of supply, along with the 2nd Libyan Division HQ and the 3rd Libyan Infantry Regiment. Combat effectiveness is, the rules inform me with clinical precision, "severely degraded." These men haven't fired a shot. The desert is killing them before the British even have to bother.

And the pasta. Six regiments without pasta. I understand, intellectually, that this is a morale modifier — that Italian infantry formations require their specific ration allocation or they suffer additional degradation. But there is something deeply, existentially absurd about sitting here at my table, consulting a chart to determine whether the 116th Infantry Regiment 'Marmarica' has received its pasta allocation for the week of September 9th, 1940, and marking "no" with genuine concern. Berg, you magnificent madman.

Twenty points of fuel evaporated this turn. Just *evaporated*. Into the Libyan air. The Tobruk depot is running low already, and I haven't moved a single mechanized unit in anger. The four Libyan infantry regiments are fuel-critical. The Maletti Group — my one decent mechanized formation on the Italian side — at least has fuel, but it's drinking water from the same empty well as everyone else.

Meanwhile, wearing my British hat: the situation is almost comically superior. Eight units, zero out of supply. The 7th Armoured Division — the Desert Rats — is sitting comfortably in the Western Desert with the 4th Armoured Brigade and 7th Armoured Brigade, fully fueled, fully watered, presumably enjoying tea. Their HQ took a step loss somehow, which concerns me; I need to check whether that's a setup condition or something I've already botched. The 4th Indian Division with its 5th and 11th Brigades rounds out the force. Small, professional, supplied. Everything the Italians are not.

The strategic picture is straightforward on paper: Graziani is supposed to advance east into Egypt. Historically, he was dragged into this offensive kicking and screaming, and now, playing his side, I completely understand why. I am looking at twenty units, three already out of supply, half the army dehydrated and pasta-deprived, fuel literally evaporating, and the supply depot at Tobruk already strained — and I'm supposed to *attack* with this? Toward Sidi Barrani, across empty desert, extending these gossamer supply lines even further?

But I must. The campaign's arc demands it. So next turn I will begin inching the 10th Army eastward and pray that the supply system doesn't collapse entirely before I reach the wire.

The duality of playing both sides is already strange. I know exactly where the 4th Armoured Brigade is. I know the Desert Rats' HQ is damaged. And yet I must pretend, on the Italian side, that I don't — must make decisions Graziani would make, blind and anxious in the heat.

Ninety-nine turns to go.

God help us all. Especially the 141st Catanzaro.
