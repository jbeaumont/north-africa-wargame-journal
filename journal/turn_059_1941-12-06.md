6 December 1941

The period opened with a rainstorm that grounded air operations and turned the desert tracks to mud for roughly the first two days. By mid-week the weather had cleared and movement resumed under normal conditions. The rain itself was not severe, but it compounded an already deteriorating Axis logistical picture in ways we are only now beginning to appreciate fully.

The principal development this turn is not a battle but a supply crisis — theirs. Intelligence and intercept reporting confirm what our forward patrols have been observing for several days: the entire Axis order of battle appears to be operating without regular supply. All thirty-four identified enemy formations are assessed as out of supply. This includes 21st Panzer Division, 32nd Ariete Division, 26th Artillery Regiment, and every reconnaissance battalion still in the field. We have seen nothing like it since the campaign began. Rommel's forces are burning through whatever local stocks they cached, and once those are exhausted they will face acute shortages of fuel, ammunition, and rations simultaneously.

The cause is straightforward. Our operations over the preceding fortnight succeeded in interdicting the coastal road at several points and pressing the Axis back from their forward dumps. The rainstorm at the start of this period further disrupted whatever ad hoc resupply convoys were still moving. The cumulative effect has been total.

On our side the picture is better but not without blemish. Six Commonwealth units remain out of supply — principally the 1st South African Division and its subordinate brigades, along with 7th Support Group and 22nd Guards Brigade. These formations are operating at the end of extended lines south and west of the main axis of advance. Corps HQ is aware of the problem and has directed transport columns forward, but fuel evaporation continues to eat into reserves at every depot. Tobruk alone lost eighty-four units of fuel to the desert heat this period; Mersa Matruh lost over a hundred. The cumulative wastage across eight depots is not trivial and reminds us that logistics in this theatre punish both sides impartially.

No major engagements were fought. With the initiative in Commonwealth hands, our commanders chose to consolidate positions and let the supply situation do its work rather than risk costly assaults against desperate formations that might still concentrate for a sharp local counterblow. The calculation is that time now favours us decisively.

At period's end the Axis force remains intact in numbers but hollow in sustainment, and if the supply interdiction holds through the next turn, we may be approaching the conditions for a general collapse across the entire Cyrenaican front.
