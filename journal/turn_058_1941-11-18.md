18 November 1941

The offensive we have been preparing for weeks — Operation Crusader — opened this morning with XXX Corps crossing the Wire south of Fort Maddalena. Eighth Army's intent is straightforward in concept if ambitious in execution: relieve Tobruk by destroying the enemy armour in the open desert between Gabr Saleh and Sidi Rezegh, then link up with the garrison. General Cunningham has the initiative, and we mean to keep it.

The first hours were marked by a sharp rainstorm that swept across the forward assembly areas. Visibility dropped, tracks turned to mud, and the going for wheeled transport became miserable. More consequentially, the rain accelerated fuel losses at every depot in the theatre. Tobruk alone lost over two hundred tons of fuel across the turn through evaporation and spillage compounded by the wet; Mersa Matruh shed nearly three hundred. Every jerrycan in the desert seems to leak a little faster when the weather turns foul, and the quartermasters are beside themselves. The storm passed by midday and conditions returned to normal for the remainder of the period, but the cumulative wastage is not trivial.

Our principal concern at turn's end is supply. Fifteen of our formations are reporting themselves out of supply, including both 7th and 4th Armoured Brigades — the very spearheads of the advance — along with 7th Support Group, 11th Infantry Brigade, and 1st South African Division. The armoured brigades pushed well ahead of the logistics tail in the opening bound, and the lorry columns have not yet caught up. Corps HQ is aware; replenishment convoys are being prioritised, but every mile south of the escarpment stretches the line thinner.

Against this, the intelligence picture offers a remarkable counterpoint. Signals intercepts and forward reconnaissance confirm that the entire Axis order of battle — all thirty-four identified formations — is currently assessed as out of supply. The interdiction of the Mediterranean convoy routes and sustained pressure on Tripoli's throughput appear to have produced a genuine logistics crisis for Rommel. The 21st Panzer Division, Ariete, and the reconnaissance battalions are all affected. Whatever reserves of fuel and ammunition remain in forward dumps cannot be reaching the fighting units in adequate quantity.

No major engagements developed during the period. Both sides are manoeuvring, and the armoured clash we expect near Sidi Rezegh has not yet materialised. The absence of an immediate Axis riposte may itself be a symptom of their supply difficulties.

The situation at the close of 18 November is one of cautious opportunity: the enemy is comprehensively starved of supply, but our own forward elements are scarcely better off, and the outcome will hinge on which side restores its logistics first.
