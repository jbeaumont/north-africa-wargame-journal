## Journal — 18 November 1941

The offensive opened today under clear skies, weather remaining normal across all three operational stages of the turn. Corps HQ had the initiative, and the intent was straightforward: press forward from the Wire positions while the Eighth Army's logistical advantage still held, and keep Rommel's forces starved of supply.

The most significant development this turn was not any single engagement but the supply picture. Intelligence and our own intercepts confirm that the entire Axis order of battle — all thirty-four formations — is now operating out of supply. The 21st Panzer Division, the Ariete, the reconnaissance battalions, even the 26th Artillery Regiment: none are drawing regularly from their depots. Tripoli, Nofilia, and el Agheila still hold fuel stocks, but the consumption-to-delivery chain appears thoroughly disrupted. Whether this owes to our interdiction efforts, the Royal Navy's pressure on the sea lanes, or simply the accumulation of distance and poor Axis logistics planning, the result is the same. Rommel's army is running on fumes.

We are not without our own difficulties. Fifteen of our thirty-six formations are similarly cut off from regular supply. The 7th and 4th Armoured Brigades, the 7th Support Group, 11th Infantry Brigade, and 1st South African Division have all outrun their supply lines or find themselves on routes not yet secured. The armoured brigades in particular concern us; they will need fuel and ammunition replenishment before any sustained armoured clash with the panzers, and the desert is unkind to stockpiles. Evaporation alone cost us nearly a hundred and fifty tons of fuel across our forward depots at Tobruk, Mersa Matruh, and Sidi Barrani over the course of the turn. The rear depots suffered proportionally. It is a quiet, relentless drain that the quartermasters track with grim diligence.

No major engagements were recorded. Both sides appear to be manoeuvring — we to develop our attack corridors toward Tobruk and the Axis frontier positions, and the enemy presumably to consolidate what little he has while awaiting resupply that may not come. The absence of fighting does not mean the absence of risk; our forward armoured units are exposed and hungry, and a concentrated Axis counterstroke against them before we restore their supply would be unwelcome.

At the close of the turn, we hold the initiative and a decisive logistical advantage, but nearly half our force is operating beyond its tether, and the campaign's first real test has yet to come.
