27 November 1941

The picture clarified considerably over the past week, and for the first time since Crusader began, one can state with some confidence that the operational balance has shifted decisively in our favour — not through any single brilliant stroke, but through the slow, grinding logic of supply.

Corps intelligence now confirms what we had suspected for several days: the entirety of the Axis force in Cyrenaica is operating without reliable supply. Every formation — 21st Panzer, the Ariete, the reconnaissance battalions, the artillery groups, all thirty-four identified units — is cut off from its depots. Rommel's aggressive forward positioning, whatever tactical dividends it may have yielded earlier, has left his army stranded. The desert is doing to him what no single engagement could accomplish so quickly.

Our own situation is not without difficulty. The 1st South African Division and its subordinate brigades remain out of supply, as do the 7th Support Group and the 22nd Guards Brigade — six formations in all that we must attend to before they lose effectiveness. But six against thirty-four is a ratio any quartermaster would accept gladly. The rear-area depots at Mersa Matruh, Sidi Barrani, and Tobruk continue to lose fuel to evaporation at roughly six per cent — an unavoidable cost of maintaining forward stocks in this climate, though the cumulative wastage is not trivial. Tobruk alone lost ninety tons of fuel this period. One wishes the engineers had better storage facilities, but there is little to be done about it now.

No major engagements occurred during this turn. The commanding general elected to maintain pressure through positioning rather than force a pitched battle. The reasoning, discussed at some length at the evening briefing, was straightforward: with the entire Axis force starving for ammunition and fuel, time works in our favour. Every day that passes without Rommel restoring his supply lines weakens him further. There is no need to accept casualties attacking formations that may soon be unable to move or fight effectively.

The South African formations remain a concern — their supply routes must be re-established promptly if we intend to use them in the next phase. Staff are working on alternative routing through Bir Khamsa, though the fuel stocks there are modest.

At the close of this period, the Eighth Army holds the initiative with thirty-six formations in the field, the weather remains cooperative, and the enemy's logistical collapse appears total and, if we act with reasonable prudence, irreversible.
