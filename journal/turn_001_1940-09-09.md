# Journal — Turn 1: Week of 9 September 1940

---

It begins. God help me, it actually begins.

I've spent three weeks just setting up — sorting counters, cross-referencing the OOB charts, trying to understand the supply tables. And now Turn 1 is on the board, and already I am drowning. Not in sand. In logistics.

Let me start with the catastrophe that is the Italian 10th Army.

Graziani's force has arrived. The 10th Army HQ is at 1405, with Cirene and Marmarica divisions deploying in the hexes around it. On paper, twenty active units. In reality? A thirsty, pasta-deprived shambles strung out along the Libyan coast. Six Italian infantry regiments — the 125th and 126th Cirene, the 115th and 116th Marmarica, and both Catanzaro regiments — are critically short of water *on the first turn*. The first turn! These men haven't even crossed into Egypt yet and they're already degraded. I stared at the water allocation tables for forty minutes trying to figure out where I went wrong, and the answer is: I didn't go wrong. There simply isn't enough. The desert doesn't care about Mussolini's timetables.

And the pasta situation. I have to write this down because no one will believe me. Six regiments are pasta-deprived. The rules are explicit: Italian infantry units that don't receive their pasta ration suffer a morale penalty. I am tracking pasta. In a wargame. The 141st Catanzaro is out of supply AND out of pasta AND critically short of water. These men are combat-ineffective before a single shot has been fired. I imagine their colonel writing furious letters to Rome.

The Libyan colonial regiments — all four of them — are fuel critical. The 2nd Libyan Division HQ, the 3rd and 4th Libyan Infantry, and the 4th Blackshirt Division are fully out of supply. The Tobruk depot is already running low. Twenty fuel points evaporated in the heat this turn. *Evaporated.* The game models fuel evaporation. I love this. I hate this.

Meanwhile, wearing my British hat: the Western Desert Force is tiny but comparatively healthy. Eight units, all in supply. But the 7th Armoured Division — my beloved Desert Rats — arrived damaged. The HQ is at half strength, the 4th and 7th Armoured Brigades are each missing a step. Water is critical even on this side of the wire; the 4th Indian Division and the 5th and 11th Indian Brigades are all feeling the shortage. But at least they have functioning supply lines. At least they have that.

The strategic picture is almost absurdly lopsided in numbers. Twenty Italian-axis units against eight Commonwealth. But Graziani's force is a paper tiger — a hollow mass of dehydrated, unfed infantry that can barely move, let alone attack. Historically, he was reluctant to advance. Playing his side, I now understand why with a visceral clarity that no history book ever gave me. You don't want to push forward when your forward units are already dying of thirst at the start line.

My plan for the Italians: consolidate. Get the supply situation sorted before even thinking about the advance toward Sidi Barrani. I need to get the Tobruk depot functioning, establish water points, and for the love of God, find some pasta.

My plan for the British: wait. Let the desert do its work. Keep the 7th Armoured's screen forward, conserve fuel, and pray that Wavell's reinforcements arrive on schedule.

It is Turn 1 of 100. I have ninety-nine more weeks of this. The counter density alone on the Axis side is already testing my table space. I've started a separate spreadsheet just for water allocation.

This game is a monster. I am in love with it.
