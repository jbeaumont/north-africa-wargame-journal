18 November 1941

Operation Crusader opened this morning. After weeks of preparation and no small amount of anxiety at Corps and Army level over timing, XXX Corps crossed the wire into Cyrenaica with the weather holding fair — clear skies, firm ground, conditions as favourable as one could ask for a November advance.

The intent is straightforward in conception if ambitious in execution: XIII Corps pins the Axis frontier garrisons while XXX Corps, spearheaded by 7th Armoured Division, swings wide through the desert south of the enemy positions to seek out and destroy the German and Italian armour, then link up with the Tobruk garrison breaking out. General Cunningham wants the armoured battle fought and won before we commit to the relief corridor. Everything hinges on whether Rommel will oblige us by concentrating his panzers where we can engage them decisively.

The advance proceeded largely without opposition through the day. 7th Armoured Brigade and 4th Armoured Brigade pushed forward aggressively, with 7th Support Group echeloned behind. 1st South African Division moved up on the left. Intelligence reports suggest we achieved genuine surprise — Axis headquarters appeared unaware of the scale of our movement until well into the afternoon. Whether that advantage holds past the first forty-eight hours is another matter entirely.

Our principal concern at this early stage is supply. Fifteen of our thirty-six units are already registering as out of supply, including nearly all the forward armoured and mobile formations — 7th Armoured Brigade, 4th Armoured Brigade, 7th Support Group, 11th Infantry Brigade, and 1st South African Division. The speed of the advance has outrun the logistics tail, and the supply columns are struggling to keep pace across the open desert. We expected this to some degree, but the proportion of forward units affected is troubling on the very first day.

Against that, the intelligence picture on the Axis side offers considerable encouragement. Every single enemy formation — all thirty-four active units — is assessed as out of supply. The interdiction campaign against Mediterranean convoys has evidently taken a far heavier toll than even our most optimistic projections suggested. 21st Panzer Division, Ariete, the reconnaissance battalions — none of them appear to have adequate fuel or ammunition reserves. If these assessments are accurate, Rommel's freedom to manoeuvre his armour in a protracted mobile battle is severely constrained.

No significant engagements occurred today; the operational stages passed with repositioning and the long columns grinding westward.

The situation at day's end is cautiously promising: we hold the initiative, we achieved surprise, and the enemy faces a supply crisis of potentially decisive proportions — though our own logistics must be brought forward urgently before the armoured clash that is surely coming.
