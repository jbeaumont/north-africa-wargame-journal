18 November 1941

Operation Crusader opened this morning. Weather held fine across the whole of Cyrenaica — clear skies, moderate temperatures, no sandstorms. Exactly what Eighth Army needed.

The broad intention, as briefed at Corps, was straightforward in concept: XXX Corps' armour would sweep wide south of the Axis frontier positions, find and destroy Rommel's panzer force, and link up with the Tobruk garrison breaking out. XIII Corps would pin the frontier garrisons in place. We held the initiative and meant to use it before the enemy could react.

Yet the first full turn has been dominated less by contact with the enemy than by the grim arithmetic of logistics. Fuel evaporation across every depot — Tobruk, Mersa Matruh, Sidi Barrani, Bir Khamsa, even Giarabub — has been relentless. At six percent per stage, compounding across three stages of operations, the cumulative losses are painful. Tobruk alone shed roughly 175 tons of fuel over the period. Mersa Matruh lost over 230. The Quartermaster's staff have been quietly furious; there is nothing to be done about the heat and the open-air storage, but it narrows every margin we have.

The supply picture, however, tells the real story of this turn. Intelligence confirms that the entire Axis order of battle — all thirty-four active formations — is currently out of supply. Twenty-first Panzer, the Ariete, the reconnaissance battalions, Rommel's artillery: none of them are drawing regular replenishment. The Mediterranean interdiction campaign and the long haul from Tripoli have evidently done their work. If this persists, Axis armour will be burning through reserves it cannot replace.

Our own situation is less comfortable than one would like for an army on the offensive. Fifteen of thirty-six formations are flagged out of supply, and these are not rear-echelon units: 7th Armoured Brigade, 4th Armoured Brigade, and 7th Support Group — the spearhead of the southern envelopment — are all running short, as is 1st South African Division and 11th Infantry Brigade. The advance has outrun the forward dumps, and the supply columns are still catching up. Corps HQ impressed upon the CRASC the urgency of getting fuel and ammunition forward before the armoured brigades make serious contact with the panzers.

No major engagements were reported. Reconnaissance elements probed forward, but the day passed without significant action — an eerie quiet that no one at headquarters expects to last.

At turn's end the strategic position favours us: we hold the initiative, the weather cooperates, and the enemy's entire force is starving for supply — but our own armoured cutting edge is worryingly dry, and everything depends on whether the logistics can keep pace with the plan.
