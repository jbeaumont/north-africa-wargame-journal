## Journal — 18 November 1941

The offensive was to have begun today. In a sense it did, though not with the thunder of armour across the wire but with a quiet, spreading paralysis that overtook both armies almost simultaneously.

Weather held fair — clear skies, moderate temperatures, no sand — yet the supply picture that emerged at morning staff conference was alarming. Fifteen of our forward formations reported their lines of communication severed or degraded beyond use. 7th Armoured Brigade, 4th Armoured Brigade, 7th Support Group, the 11th Hussars — the very tip of our offensive spear — all flagged out of supply before midday. The 1st South African Division and its constituent brigades likewise. Even 2nd New Zealand Division and 4th Indian Division, whom we had thought tolerably positioned, lost contact with their supply heads. By the second operational phase these same units had deteriorated to critical status, a rate of decline that took the Q staff by surprise.

The cause appears to be a combination of factors: forward dumps not yet pushed far enough west, the extended approach march consuming more fuel and water than estimated, and convoy routing through the desert tracks proving less efficient than the planners had assumed. Fuel evaporation from the depot at Mersa Matruh alone cost us over three hundred tons across the period — a persistent drain that compounds every other shortage.

What gave us pause, however, was the intelligence picture of the enemy. Signals intercepts and reconnaissance confirmed that the Axis supply situation was not merely bad but catastrophic. Every single enemy formation — all thirty-four active units — appears to have fallen out of supply and then into critical status within the same period. Both Panzer divisions, the Ariete, 90th Light, the Trento, Bologna, Brescia, Pavia — the entire order of battle from frontline armour down to the Oasis battalion companies garrisoning the southern flank. Rommel's forces are, for the moment, immobilised and starving for fuel and ammunition.

Corps HQ debated whether to press the attack immediately despite our own shortages, reasoning that the enemy's condition was far worse. The counterargument — that committing formations already at critical supply into battle risked their destruction for marginal gain — carried the day. No major ground actions were ordered. The turn passed without combat.

Instead, the logisticians were given priority. Every available truck column was redirected to restoring the supply chain to our forward divisions, with particular urgency placed on 7th Armoured Division's headquarters and its three brigades. If we can feed even half our striking force back to adequate supply before the enemy recovers his own, the operational advantage will be decisive.

At turn's end both armies sit immobilised in the desert, hollowed out by logistics rather than gunfire — but with every Axis unit at critical supply against only fifteen of ours, time favours us if we use it wisely.
