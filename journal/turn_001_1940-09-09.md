# Journal — Turn 1: 9 September 1940

---

The war in North Africa begins not with a thunderclap but with a logistics spreadsheet and the slow, grinding sound of my own confidence evaporating — at roughly the same rate as my fuel, apparently. Twenty point three fuel points, gone to the Libyan sun before a single engine turns over in anger. I stared at that number for a long time. The desert takes its cut before anyone else.

I am Graziani and Wavell both. I am the man who must advance and the man who must hold. This is the peculiar madness of solo play: I know exactly what I'm planning on both sides, and yet somehow still manage to surprise myself with how badly things are going.

Let me start with the Italian situation, because frankly it's the one keeping me up at night.

The 10th Army HQ has arrived at hex 1405, and with it the 63rd Division "Cirene" and the 62nd "Marmarica," filing onto the map like men arriving at a party where there is no food, no water, and no one told them where the bathroom is. Six infantry regiments — the 125th and 126th Cirene, the 115th and 116th Marmarica, the 141st and 142nd Catanzaro — are all critically short of water. *Water.* Not ammunition, not fuel, not some exotic spare part. Water. The most basic substance required for human survival, and I cannot deliver it reliably to units that have barely left the staging area.

And the pasta. God, the pasta. Six regiments are pasta-deprived. I keep returning to this rule with a mixture of horror and admiration: Italian infantry suffer actual combat effectiveness penalties if they don't receive their pasta ration. Berg, you magnificent lunatic. The 125th Cirene is out there somewhere east of Tobruk, dehydrated, unfed, and presumably furious. The Marmarica boys are no better off. Catanzaro's 141st is fully out of supply — no fuel trace, no water, no pasta, nothing. They exist on the map as a counter, but operationally they're a cluster of angry, thirsty men sitting in the sand wondering why Rome sent them here.

The Libyan colonial regiments — 1st through 4th — are all fuel-critical. The 2nd Libyan Division HQ is out of supply entirely. The Maletti Group, my one decent mechanized asset on the Axis side, at least has fuel but is water-critical. Everyone is water-critical. The Tobruk depot is running low and I haven't moved a single unit forward yet.

Meanwhile, wearing my British hat: the 7th Armoured Division — the Desert Rats — sits with a damaged HQ and the 4th Armoured Brigade at two-thirds strength. Not ideal, but compared to the Italians, Wavell's little force is a model of organizational competence. Zero units out of supply. The 4th Indian Division with the 5th and 11th Brigades is intact and ready. Seven units against twenty, and honestly, I'd take those odds as the British without hesitation.

My strategic dilemma is absurd. As Graziani, Mussolini expects me to advance toward Sidi Barrani, then Egypt. With what? My supply lines are already failing at the start hex. As Wavell, I need only wait — the desert itself is fighting for me. But I know (wearing both hats simultaneously, this schizophrenic general) that if the Italians don't push now, they never will. The British reinforcement pipeline will bury them.

So next turn, I think I must try to consolidate Italian supply. Everything runs through Tobruk, and Tobruk is not enough. I need to get water forward, establish intermediate dumps, and accept that any advance will be glacial. Ninety-nine turns remain. The distance to Alexandria might as well be measured in water tins.

I spent forty minutes on the supply phase alone today. Forty minutes, and no one moved, no one fought, and six Italian regiments went hungry and dry.

I am beginning to understand why this game takes 1,500 hours.
