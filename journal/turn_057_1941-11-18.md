18 November 1941

The offensive opened this morning under grey skies, though the weather initially held. Eighth Army crossed the wire in strength, and for the first hours the enemy appeared unaware that Crusader had begun. Corps HQ's principal concern was not contact with the enemy but logistics — and on that score the picture that emerged over the course of the day was extraordinary.

Our advance had been designed in part to sever the Axis supply network running along the coast, and the intelligence summaries reaching us by mid-morning confirmed that the interdiction effort — combined with sustained pressure from the Royal Navy and RAF on the sea lanes — had taken hold far more completely than anyone had dared hope. By the time the first supply assessments were compiled, every single Axis formation in Cyrenaica had been flagged as out of supply. Not merely forward elements or isolated garrisons: both Panzer divisions, the 90th Light, the Ariete, the Trento, Bologna, Brescia, and Pavia — the lot. Even the small Oasis companies and the artillery Raggruppamenti behind the Tobruk perimeter reported critical shortages. Thirty-four units, without exception, slid from out-of-supply to critical status within the same operational period. One has to assume Rommel's depots were already threadbare before we moved; our advance simply cut the last threads.

We were not immune to the same affliction. The 1st South African Division, 1st and 5th South African Brigades, 7th Support Group, and 22nd Guards Brigade all lost contact with their supply heads during the advance. The rapidity of the move across the frontier left forward dumps behind, and the road construction battalion attached to the South Africans went critical alongside its parent formation. Six of our units affected — an annoyance, but manageable against the scale of the Axis collapse.

No significant engagements developed. Whether the enemy chose not to react or simply could not is unclear, though given the state of his fuel and ammunition stocks the latter seems more probable. We had the initiative and intended to exploit it, but a violent rainstorm broke over the theatre during the afternoon, turning the desert tracks to mud and grounding air support. Movement effectively ceased.

The absence of combat in the opening turn is mildly frustrating — we had hoped to strike before Rommel grasped what was happening — but the supply picture more than compensates. An army cannot fight on empty and the Axis force is, at this moment, comprehensively starving across every category of supply. If the rain lifts and we can close rapidly, we may find an enemy already half-beaten before the first serious blow lands.
