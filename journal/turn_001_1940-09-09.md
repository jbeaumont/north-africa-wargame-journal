# Journal Entry — Turn 1, Week of 9 September 1940

---

The war in North Africa begins not with a bang but with a logistics spreadsheet.

I've spent the better part of this evening staring at the Italian order of battle and trying to understand how Marshal Graziani is supposed to invade Egypt when half his army can't find a drink of water. The 10th Army HQ has just arrived at hex 1405 along with the 63rd "Cirene" Division, and already — *already*, before a single shot is fired — the 125th and 126th Regiments of Cirene are critically short of water. They just got here. They've barely unpacked. The Marmarica division's regiments, the 115th and 116th, same story. The Catanzaro boys, the 141st and 142nd? Also parched. And the 141st is fully out of supply, along with the 2nd Libyan Division HQ and the 3rd Libyan Infantry Regiment.

I keep checking the rules to see if I've done something wrong. I haven't. This is simply what it means to field nineteen units in the Libyan desert when your supply infrastructure runs through the bottleneck of Tobruk, which is already running low. Twenty point three fuel points evaporated into the September heat. Just… gone. The desert took them. I didn't move a single truck.

And then there's the pasta.

Six Italian infantry regiments — the entirety of the Cirene, Marmarica, and Catanzaro combat regiments — are without their pasta rations this week. I understand this is a morale and effectiveness modifier, and I understand that Richard Berg included this rule because Italian logistical doctrine genuinely required pasta preparation with its attendant water costs, and I understand all of this intellectually. But there is something profoundly humbling about sitting here as the de facto commander of the Italian 10th Army, watching my offensive capability degrade before the campaign even starts because I cannot boil noodles. Rommel isn't even here yet. The enemy I'm losing to is thermodynamics.

Speaking of the enemy — wearing my British hat now — the situation across the wire is almost comically more manageable. Seven units, all in supply. The 7th Armoured Division, the Desert Rats, has taken some wear on its HQ (half a step gone, which worries me), but the 4th and 7th Armoured Brigades are intact, the 4th Royal Horse Artillery is ready, and the 4th Indian Division with its 5th and 11th Brigades is sitting comfortably. Even *they* are water-critical, which tells me something about the baseline misery of this theatre, but at least no one is out of supply. No one is missing dinner.

The strategic picture is what history dictates: Graziani must advance east toward Sidi Barrani, and I must make him do it despite knowing, as the Allied player, exactly where every gap in his logistics chain sits. The duality of solo play in this game is genuinely strange. I am trying to make the Italian invasion succeed while simultaneously planning how to make it fail. The best I can do is play each side honestly, make the decisions that commander would make with the information that commander would have.

For now, the immediate Axis priority is brutally clear: fix supply before anything moves. The Tobruk depot needs reinforcement. I need to establish a water distribution chain that can actually reach the forward divisions. The Libyan regiments are fuel-critical on top of everything else — the 1st through 4th Libyan Infantry Regiments can barely move.

On the British side, I wait. Let the desert do its work. Keep the Desert Rats' fuel reserves intact for when they'll matter. Watch.

It is Turn 1 of 100. Ninety-nine weeks remain. The Italians can't make pasta. God help me.
