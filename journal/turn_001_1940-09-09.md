# Journal — Turn 1: Week of 9 September 1940

---

It begins.

Graziani's 10th Army has materialized on the map — the army HQ settling at hex 1405, with the 63rd Division "Cirene" and 62nd Division "Marmarica" filing in along the coastal road east of Tobruk. Twenty Italian and Libyan units in theater. The paper strength looks formidable. The reality is something else entirely.

I've been staring at the supply tables for the better part of an hour, and the picture is genuinely alarming. Every single Italian infantry regiment on the map — Cirene's 125th and 126th, Marmarica's 115th and 116th, Catanzaro's 141st and 142nd — is critically short of water. *All of them.* These men haven't even begun the advance toward Sidi Barrani and they're already degraded. The September heat is punishing, and the Tobruk Port Supply Depot is running low. Twenty points of fuel simply evaporated this turn — gone into the Libyan air, doing nothing, achieving nothing, lost to thermodynamics and bad storage.

Three units are fully out of supply: the 141st Regiment "Catanzaro," the 2nd Libyan Division HQ, and the 3rd Libyan Infantry Regiment. Turn *one*. We haven't fired a shot.

And then there's the pasta.

Six Italian regiments — the combat core of three divisions — are pasta-deprived. I keep turning this over in my mind. In any other wargame this would be a joke, a footnote, a curiosity for BGG threads. Here it is a real morale modifier with real consequences. These are conscript infantry who were promised at minimum a hot meal of pasta as part of the daily ration, and the supply chain has already failed them. The 125th Cirene is thirsty, hungry, and demoralized, and the campaign is six days old. Berg, you magnificent bastard.

Wearing my British hat now: the situation is almost embarrassingly comfortable by comparison. Seven units — 4th Armoured Brigade, 7th Armoured Brigade, 4th Royal Horse Artillery, 4th Indian Division HQ with its 5th and 11th Indian Infantry Brigades — all fully supplied. Water critical, yes, because this is the Western Desert and *everyone* is water critical, but nothing out of supply. The 7th Armoured is the sharp end of the stick. I need to keep them mobile, aggressive, harassing. Time is on Britain's side right now.

The strategic calculus is stark. Historically, Graziani advanced reluctantly on Mussolini's direct orders and stopped at Sidi Barrani, where Wavell's Operation Compass shattered him in December. Playing both sides, I feel the tension viscerally: as the Italians, I *must* advance or the campaign dies, but advancing means stretching an already broken supply line deeper into the desert. As the British, I want to conserve, screen, and wait for the moment to strike.

My immediate Italian priority: fix the supply situation before any offensive movement. Route water forward from Tobruk. Get the Libyan divisions their fuel. Somehow find pasta — I need to trace supply from the port through to the forward depots and figure out where the bottleneck is. The Catanzaro regiment being fully out of supply in the rear area suggests a depot allocation error on my part; I may have miscounted the draw during the logistics phase.

The Maletti Group sits ready — the one semi-capable mechanized formation the Italians possess. Medium tanks, motorized infantry. I'm tempted to push it forward as a screen, but not until I can guarantee its fuel.

Ninety-nine turns to go. I've already spent more time on water tables than on any tactical decision. This, I suspect, is the game working exactly as intended.
