18 November 1941

The day we had been waiting for. At first light, XXX Corps launched the opening phase of Operation Crusader, pushing south and west into the desert with the objective of relieving Tobruk and destroying Rommel's armour in the process. Weather held fair throughout — no sandstorms, no rain — which was all General Cunningham could have asked for given the distances involved.

The plan, as rehearsed, called for 7th Armoured Division to sweep wide through the desert, drawing the Axis armour into a decisive engagement while XIII Corps pinned the frontier garrisons. The first hours went quietly. Our forward elements — 11th Hussars, the armoured brigades, 7th Support Group — pressed forward without significant contact. The surprise appeared genuine; Panzergruppe Afrika had not anticipated the timing of our advance.

What consumed most of the staff's attention today was not enemy action but the supply picture. The advance immediately strained our logistics. Central India Horse Regiment and 1st Army Tank Brigade both dropped out of supply early in the day as columns stretched across unfamiliar ground, though both were restored within hours as the supply echelons caught up. The 4th South African Armoured Car Battalion, operating on the flanks, lost supply contact and by day's end had deteriorated to critical status — a concern, given their screening role. Several formations that had been nursing critical supply levels during the build-up period — 7th Armoured Brigade, 4th Armoured Brigade, 22nd Armoured Brigade, the Guards Brigade, and the South African brigades — finally drew full resupply as the depots opened and the advance routes were formalised. HQ 7th Armoured Division itself had been operating on fumes and only regained proper supply during the afternoon.

The more striking intelligence, confirmed by intercepts and patrol reports, was the state of the enemy. Every Axis formation we could identify — all thirty-four units, from 21st Panzer Division down to the reconnaissance battalions — appeared to be operating without adequate supply. The Ariete, the 33rd and 3rd Aufklärung battalions, the 26th Artillery Regiment: all starved of fuel and ammunition. Whether this reflected interdiction of the Mediterranean convoys, poor distribution forward, or simply Rommel's characteristic overextension, we could not yet say. But it was an extraordinary advantage to begin an offensive against a force entirely out of supply.

No significant engagements were reported. The day belonged to movement and logistics, not fire.

At the close of 18 November, the offensive has begun under conditions more favourable than we had reason to expect, though the true test will come when our armour meets the panzers and we discover whether their supply deficit translates into battlefield weakness.
